A semiconductor is a type of material where the distance between the valence and conduction bands is small enough that the electrons can jump from one to the other at about room temperature. However, a pure semiconductor is not that useful which bring us to...

### Doping
This is the process of adding an impurity to the atomic lattice of a semiconductor to either add one extra electron or take one away. Depending on which one is used, the resulting material can be categories into two groups:

### n-type
In n-type semiconductors there is an *extra* electron present. For example, the case with silicon, it could look like this:
<img src="Pasted image 20260402133530.png" width="300" style="display:block; margin:auto;">
The phosphorus atom brings one negative charged electron, hence the name. Also forms a *donor* level just below the conduction band to which electrons from the valence band can jump to

### p-type
p-type semiconductors *lack* one electron. For example, the same case with silicon would look like this:
<img src="Pasted image 20260402133751.png" width="300" style="display:block; margin:auto;">
The boron atom has one electron less than the surrounding Si atoms which makes it positive, hence the name. Also adds a *acceptor* level just above the valence band

### n-type + p-type
By putting two materials together electrons start flowing a little by themselves, without any current applied:
<img src="Pasted image 20260402134222.png" width="600" style="display:block; margin:auto;">
Which creates a little electric field. And when that field becomes too strong the electrons stop flowing by forming a *depletion region*: a piece of material where there're no charges moving

### Let there be light!
The energy required for the electron to cross over from n to p can be emitted as light (the basics of LED) and the size of the band gap determines the color of emitted light. More on that in [[LED]]
