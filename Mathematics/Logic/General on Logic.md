A *functionally complete* set of operands is such a set that can do anything else of any complexity: $\{\neg, \ \land, \ \lor\}$ is functionally complete (as well as $\{\neg, \ \land\}$)

Any formal language (logic is of course also a formal language) has:
- **Alphabet**: the atomic parts of the language
- **Syntax**: rules by which those parts can be ordered to say something
- **Semantic**: does that what is said have meaning?

To settle this once and for all:
- -> is [[Implication]] and => is just a meta-symbol that says that whatever's to the left is the same as to the right but one-way transformation
- <-> is logical equivalence and <=> is again a meta-symbol meaning that whatever's to the left is the same to the right and is equivalent to it, two-way transformation ($\equiv$ is just a stronger version of <=>)
